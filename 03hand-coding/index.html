Tips & Tricks

Practicing voice acting is essential because it builds the vocal control, emotional range, and performance consistency needed to succeed in a competitive industry.
Just like any other form of acting or performance, voice work demands regular training to strengthen your skills, expand your versatility, and develop muscle memory
for clear articulation, character work, and stamina. Ongoing practice also helps you stay sharp for auditions, adapt quickly to direction, and deliver high-quality
performances with confidence and professionalism.


What Should You Practice?

1. Vocal Warm-ups

  • Do lip trills, tongue twisters, and breathing exercises daily.
  • Warm up your pitch, tone, and resonance before sessions.
  • Helps prevent strain and improve vocal control.

2. Diction and Articulation

  • Practice tongue twisters to improve clarity.
  • Focus on clean consonant sounds (e.g., plosives and fricatives).
  • Record and review to catch slurred or lazy speech.

3. Character Voices

  • Develop distinct voices by age, personality, and accent.
  • Practice consistency, especially for long-form content.
  • Work on switching quickly between characters.

4. Cold Reading

  • Read unfamiliar scripts aloud on the spot.
  • Focus on pacing, emotion, and flow without preparation.
  • Improves adaptability in auditions and directed sessions.

5. Listening and Mimicry

  • Imitate voices from ads, films, and games to build range.
  • Practice matching tone, rhythm, and inflection.
  • Record side-by-side comparisons with originals.

6. Acting Techniques

  • Study emotional delivery without relying on facial expressions.
  • Rehearse different emotional beats and reactions.
  • Use scripts, monologues, or improv to explore character depth.

7. Microphone Technique

  • Learn how to control distance and avoid plosives or popping.
  • Practice staying on-mic while performing physically or emotionally.
  • Experiment with positioning to find the sweet spot for your voice.

8. Script Analysis

  • Break down scripts for tone, subtext, and intent.
  • Mark up scripts with notes for pacing, emphasis, and emotion.
  • Adapt your delivery to the style and audience of the piece.

9. Accents and Dialects

  • Study common accents (e.g., British, Southern US, General American).
  • Use phonetic tools like the IPA or dialect archives (e.g., IDEA).
  • Practice regularly to retain accuracy and consistency.

10. Business and Audition Skills

  • Practice self-direction and auditioning with minimal prep.
  • Learn how to slate and deliver multiple takes with variety.
  • Understand branding, networking, and how to market yourself.

How to Make A Demo Reel

1. Choose a Genre

  • Pick one genre per reel: Commercial, Animation, Video Games, Narration, Audiobooks, or Promo.
  • Don't mix genres in a single reel.

2. Select or Write Scripts

  • Use short clips (10–15 seconds each).
  • Showcase variety in tone, energy, and style.
  • Stay within your natural vocal range and casting type.

3. Record Professionally

  • Use a high-quality microphone and a treated recording space.
  • Record clean, noise-free audio.
  • Consider hiring a professional demo producer for best results.

4. Edit and Mix

  • Keep the reel between 45–60 seconds.
  • Start with your strongest clip to grab attention.
  • Add light music or SFX only if it enhances the performance.
  • Ensure transitions are smooth and the pacing is tight.

5. Get Feedback

  • Share your reel with coaches, peers, or industry professionals.
  • Revise based on constructive feedback.

6. Use It

  • Upload your demo to casting sites, your website, and social platforms.
  • Include it in agent submissions and job applications.

Pro Tips

  • Never use copyrighted scripts or character lines.
  • Don’t include impressions unless the reel is for parody or impersonation work.
  • Update your reel every 1–2 years or as your skills evolve.
  • Cut anything that’s not your best — every clip should shine.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Poor Mic Technique

  • Too close or far from the mic, causing popping or volume issues.
  • Not using a pop filter or recording in a treated space.

2. Flat or Unnatural Delivery

  • Reading instead of performing.
  • Sounding monotone or overacting.
  • Lack of emotional connection or intent.

3. Bad Demo Reels

  • Too long (keep it under 60 seconds).
  • Weak material or poor audio quality.
  • Mixing unrelated genres in one reel.

4. Lack of Preparation

  • Skipping vocal warm-ups.
  • Not analyzing the script before recording.
  • Submitting auditions without practice or polish.

5. Ignoring the Business Side

  • Missing deadlines or not following directions.
  • No professional email, website, or portfolio.
  • Not networking or marketing yourself properly.

6. Avoiding Feedback and Growth

  • Resisting coaching or critique.
  • Not improving your skills over time.
  • Sticking only to what you're comfortable with.

Using this information helps you focus on the core skills and habits that make a successful voice actor. By avoiding common mistakes, practicing consistently,
and building a strong demo reel, you create a solid foundation for both creative and professional growth. Applying these tips will not only improve your vocal technique
and performance but also increase your chances of booking work, impressing casting directors, and standing out in a crowded field.
